Why the Strait of Hormuz matters

It is a narrow maritime passage between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf with the Gulf of Oman and the Arabian Sea.

A critical point for global energy

The U.S. Energy Information Administration describes it as the world's most important oil transit chokepoint. A disruption can delay supplies, raise transport costs and increase energy price volatility.

The EIA also reported that in 2024 roughly one-fifth of global liquefied natural gas trade passed through the strait, primarily from Qatar.

It is not only about oil

Navigational safety

Mines, attacks, congestion and military activity may affect crews, insurance and routes even when the passage remains technically open.

Trade and food security

Ships carry energy and other goods. The IMO has warned about consequences for trade, food security and thousands of seafarers.

Prices and markets

Markets react not only to an effective closure, but also to disruption probability, insurance costs and rerouting.

Diplomacy and maritime law

Freedom of navigation and the legal regime of international straits are central to the diplomatic and legal debate.

Open does not mean safe

The site deliberately separates two questions: is there an operational route? and is the area low risk? It may show OPEN WITH RESTRICTIONS when transit continues alongside incidents or special instructions.

For operational information, always consult the official maritime organisations.
